Introduction to Chemistry

Chemistry is often called the "central science" because it bridges physics, biology, and environmental sciences, providing essential insights into the composition, structure, and behavior of matter. It is the study of atoms and molecules—the building blocks of everything in the universe—and the changes they undergo, known as chemical reactions. By examining how substances interact, chemistry explains everything from the metabolic processes in living organisms to the creation of new materials. It is not merely abstract theory; it is a practical discipline applied in daily life, ranging from understanding cooking to manufacturing medicines.

1. Atomic Structure

Atoms are made of protons (positive), neutrons (neutral), and electrons (negative). The number of protons determines the element.

  [Nucleus: protons & neutrons]
  Electrons orbit around nucleus

2. Periodic Table

  • Elements are arranged by atomic number.
  • Groups (columns) share properties.
  • Periods (rows) show energy levels.

3. Chemical Reactions

  • Reactants → Products
  • Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O
  • Balance equations so atoms are equal on both sides.
Example Reaction:
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

4. Acids and Bases

  • Acids: pH less than 7 (e.g., lemon juice)
  • Bases: pH greater than 7 (e.g., soap)
  • Neutral: pH = 7 (pure water)

5. Chemistry Vocabulary

  • Catalyst: Speeds up a reaction
  • Compound: Two or more elements bonded
  • Molecule: Smallest unit of a compound

Tip: Practice balancing equations and use periodic table charts for reference!

Study Guide

  • Review atomic structure and periodic table basics. Study electron configuration, isotopes, ions, and periodic trends (electronegativity, atomic radius, ionization energy).
  • Practice balancing chemical equations. Learn about types of reactions: synthesis, decomposition, single/double replacement, combustion, and redox reactions.
  • Summarize key reactions (acid-base, redox). Study pH, titration, buffers, and electrochemistry. Explore organic chemistry: hydrocarbons, functional groups, polymers, and biomolecules.
  • Use flashcards for element names and symbols. Expand to include chemical formulas, polyatomic ions, and common compounds.
  • Draw diagrams for molecular structures. Learn Lewis structures, VSEPR theory, hybridization, and molecular geometry.
  • Study thermodynamics: energy, enthalpy, entropy, Gibbs free energy, and heat transfer. Practice calculating reaction rates and equilibrium.
  • Explore laboratory techniques: safety, measurement, titration, chromatography, spectroscopy, and qualitative/quantitative analysis.
  • Read scientific articles and summarize findings. Practice critical thinking, evaluating sources, and interpreting experimental data.
  • Explore current topics: nanotechnology, green chemistry, environmental chemistry, and advances in medicine and materials science.
