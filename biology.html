Introduction to Biology

Biology is the scientific study of life and living organisms, encompassing their structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, and distribution. Derived from the Greek words bios (life) and logos (study), this broad field ranges from the molecular machinery within cells to the complex interactions of entire ecosystems. Biologists explore the diversity of life, from microscopic bacteria to towering trees and complex animals, including humans. Key concepts include cell theory, genetics, evolution, ecology, and physiology. Biology is essential for understanding health, disease, environmental issues, and the interconnectedness of all living things on Earth.

1. Cell Structure

All living things are made of cells. Cells can be prokaryotic (no nucleus, like bacteria) or eukaryotic (with nucleus, like plants and animals).

  • Cell membrane: Controls what enters and leaves the cell.
  • Nucleus: Contains DNA/genetic material (in eukaryotes).
  • Mitochondria: Produces energy for the cell.
  • Chloroplast: (plants only) Site of photosynthesis.

2. Genetics

DNA is the molecule that carries genetic information. Genes are segments of DNA that code for traits. Heredity is the passing of traits from parents to offspring.

  • Chromosomes: Structures made of DNA.
  • Gene: A section of DNA coding for a trait.
  • Genotype: Genetic makeup; Phenotype: Physical traits.

3. Photosynthesis

Plants make food using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide. The process is called photosynthesis.

Equation:
CO₂ + H₂O + sunlight → Glucose + O₂
  • Occurs in chloroplasts.
  • Produces glucose (food) and oxygen.

4. Ecology

Ecology is the study of how living things interact with each other and their environment.

  • Food web: Shows how energy moves through organisms.
  • Producers: Make their own food (plants).
  • Consumers: Eat other organisms.
  • Decomposers: Break down dead material.
Example Food Web:

5. Key Vocabulary

  • Mitosis: Cell division for growth.
  • Osmosis: Movement of water across a membrane.
  • Autotroph: Makes own food.
  • Heterotroph: Eats other organisms.

Tip: Draw diagrams and use flashcards to help memorize terms!

Study Guide

  • Draw and label cell diagrams (prokaryotic/eukaryotic). Include details for each organelle: cell membrane, nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplast, ribosome, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosome, and cytoplasm. Compare plant, animal, and bacterial cells.
  • Summarize organelle functions and genetics basics. Explain DNA structure, replication, transcription, translation, and gene regulation. Describe Mendelian genetics, Punnett squares, dominant/recessive traits, and genetic disorders.
  • Practice explaining photosynthesis and respiration. Write out the full equations, describe the steps of light-dependent and light-independent reactions, and compare aerobic vs anaerobic respiration. Include diagrams of chloroplast and mitochondria.
  • Use flashcards for vocabulary (mitosis, osmosis, etc). Expand to include terms like meiosis, homeostasis, autotroph, heterotroph, symbiosis, adaptation, evolution, biodiversity, and ecological succession.
  • Review food webs and ecological relationships. Draw complex food webs, identify producers, consumers, decomposers, and explain energy flow. Study biomes, population dynamics, carrying capacity, and human impact on ecosystems.
  • Study the scientific method: hypothesis, experiment, observation, analysis, conclusion. Practice designing experiments and interpreting data.
  • Explore current topics: CRISPR, genetic engineering, biotechnology, climate change, conservation biology, and medical advances.
  • Read scientific articles and summarize findings. Practice critical thinking and evaluating sources.
